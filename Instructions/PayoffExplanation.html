In the following experiment, your bonus payoff will depend on how close you are to the optimal threshold θopt, that is the threshold that minimizes overall costs.
In our previous example, the AI algorithm predicted negative and positive characteristics out of a set of 20 samples. Let us assume that the optimal threshold θopt is different from our previously chosen threshold θ. Now, with θopt we expect the following outcomes, also displayed in the confusion matrix below:

  • 12 correct predictions: 4 True Positives, 8 True Negatives
  • 8 incorrect predictions: 2 False Positives and 6 False Negatives

Based thereon, for the optimal threshold θopt we would expect 2 x 5$ (=10$) for False Positive Predictions and 6 x 1$ (=6$) cost units for False Negative predictions, resulting in an optimum of 16$ overall. We display the overall costs in the cost matrix below. Hence, we expect less overall costs than with the previously chosen threshold (19$).
Note that the overall number of correct predictions (12) with the optimal threshold θopt is below the number of correct predictions (13) with our previous threshold choice. This is acceptable since our objective is to minimize overall costs.

How your bonus payoff gets calculated:

For every cost unit that your selected threshold is worse to the optimal cost (based on the optimal threshold θopt), your bonus per round (100 units) will be reduced by 1 unit, but not below 0 units. In the example above, our threshold leads to costs of 19$ while the optimum is at 16$ (a difference of 3$). Therefore, our bonus after this round would be 97 units (100 - 3) before we move on to the next round.
After the last round, your cumulated bonus payoffs will be translated to real money, with 100 units equal to 1€. In the example above, we would get 97ct for this round. Together with the bonus payoffs from all other rounds, this amount is added to the participation fee of 5€.
