In the following experiment, your task will be to choose a decision threshold in multiple rounds for different prediction scenarios. Your bonus payoff will depend on how close your chosen threshold is to the optimal threshold θopt, that is the threshold that minimizes overall costs.

Let us illustrate the bonus payoff calculation again with our previous example. The AI algorithm predicted negative and positive characteristics out of a set of 20 samples, where 10 samples have the positive and 10 samples have the negative characteristic. Costs for misclassifications were 5$ for each False Positive (FP) prediction and 1$ for each False Negative (FN) prediction, respectively. With our selected threshold, the algorithm made 13 correct predictions (accuracy 0.65). Moreover, misclassification costs were 3 x 5$ (=15$) for False Positive predictions and 4 x 1$ (=4$) for False Negative predictions, resulting in 19$ overall.

Now, let us assume that the optimal threshold θopt is different from our previously chosen threshold θ. In fact, with θopt we expect the following outcomes, also displayed in the confusion matrix below:

  • 12 correct predictions: 4 True Positives, 8 True Negatives
  • 8 incorrect predictions: 2 False Positives and 6 False Negatives

For the optimal threshold θopt we would expect misclassification costs of 2 x 5$ (=10$) for False Positive predictions and 6 x 1$ (=6$) for False Negative predictions, resulting in 16$ overall. As we can see, we expect less overall costs than with the previously chosen threshold (19$). Comparing all possible thresholds θ and their resulting misclassification costs, we find that 16$ is the lowest number possible (the optimum) and therefore the corresponding threshold is named θopt. Note that the overall number of correct predictions with the optimal threshold θopt is 12 (accuracy 0.60) and therefore below the number of correct predictions (13, accuracy 0.65) with our previous threshold choice. Despite θopt being suboptimal regarding accuracy, remember that the optimal threshold θopt is always minimizing costs in our setting.

How your bonus payoff gets calculated:

In each round, you can earn a bonus payoff in addition to your participation fee. For every cost unit that your threshold selection is above the cost minimum (achieved with the optimal threshold θopt), your bonus payoff per round (100 cents) will be reduced by 1 cent, but won't get reduced below 0 cents (no risk of negative payoff).
In the example above, our threshold selection leads to costs of 19$ while the minimum achieved with θopt is at 16$ (a difference of 3$). Therefore, our bonus payoff after this round would be 97 cents (100 - 3) before we move on to the next round. Together with the bonus payoffs from all other rounds, this amount is added to the participation fee of 5€.
