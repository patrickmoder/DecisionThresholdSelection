Before applying the AI algorithm in a real-world setting, the balance between breakdown- and no-breakdown-predictions has to be calibrated.

For each machine, the AI algorithm calculates a breakdown probability in the next 48 hours. The probability output by the AI algorithm is then translated into a binary (yes/no) prediction: if the probability is above a threshold D, we predict a breakdown in the next 48 hours; while if the probability is below the threshold D, we predict no breakdown in the next 48 hours. The threshold D has to be between 0 and 1. You, as the operations manager, have to set the threshold D to calibrate the AI algorithm. The selection of D affects the following trade-off:

  • The smaller the threshold D, the more often breakdowns will be predicted (i.e., machines with lower breakdown probabilities are predicted to break down).
  • The larger the threshold D, the less often breakdowns will be predicted (i.e., only machines with higher breakdown probabilities are predicted to break down).
