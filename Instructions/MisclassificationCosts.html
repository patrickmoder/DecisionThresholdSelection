When making incorrect predictions of machine breakdowns, we have different misclassification costs. Such misclassification costs can vary and depend on the environment to which the AI algorithm is applied:

  • After False Alarms, we face unnecessary maintenance activities that cause unnecessary down-times of machines.
  • After Missed Hits, we lose the opportunity to prevent the breakdown and face costly down-times of machines.

Let us illustrate these considerations in an example: In our example, we assume misclassification costs of 5 cost units for each False Alarm and 1 cost unit for each Missed Hit, respectively. That means that wrongly predicting a machine's breakdown is five times more expensive than wrongly predicting a machine not to break down. In the training set that is used to train the AI algorithm, out of a sample of 20 machines, 10 break down in the next 48 hours and 10 machines do not break down.
Now, let us assume that we choose a decision threshold D of 0.65, which determines that for all predicted breakdown probabilities above a value of 0.65, we predict the machine to break down. With D = 0.65, the predictions by the AI algorithm for the 20 machines in the training set (also displayed in the matrix below) would be as follows:

  • 13 correct predictions: 6 Correct Predictions of Breakdown, 7 Correct Predictions of No Breakdown
  • 7 incorrect predictions: 3 False Alarms and 4 Missed Hits

Prediction Outcomes
Predicted State
Breakdown No Breakdown
Actual State Breakdown 6 4
No Breakdown 3 7

Consequently, on our training set we would expect misclassification costs of
3 x 5 cost units (=15 cost units) for False Alarms
+ 4 x 1 cost unit (=4 cost units) for Missed Hits = 19 cost units overall
